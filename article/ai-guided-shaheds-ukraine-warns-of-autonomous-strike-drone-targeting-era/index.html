AI-Guided Shaheds: Ukraine Warns of Autonomous Strike Drone Targeting Era

A senior Ukrainian drone warfare advisor says Russia's Shahed-class loitering munitions are on the verge of selecting and striking targets on their own, with no human in the loop.

SECTOR · MIDDLE EAST PUBLISHED · 2026-06-26

Ukraine's Drone Advisor Warns Autonomous Strike Era Is Close

Serhii 'Flash' Beskrestnov, an advisor to Ukraine's defence ministry on unmanned systems, has a blunt message: the next generation of Shahed-class loitering munitions won't need a human to pick their targets. Publishing his assessment this week, Beskrestnov argues that autonomous target selection isn't a distant prospect — it's an imminent reality for the entire class of one-way attack drones, with consequences that stretch well beyond Eastern Europe into the Middle East.

From Pre-Programmed Paths to Fully Autonomous Decision-Making

Right now, Russia fires its domestically produced Shahed derivatives — designated Gerans — in mass salvos against Ukrainian cities, flight paths loaded before launch. But Moscow has been steadily upgrading these weapons: anti-jamming systems, rear-facing cameras that can detect and evade interceptors, hardware for remote human piloting mid-flight. Beskrestnov's reading of that trajectory is straightforward. The logical endpoint is a drone that scans a target area, weighs priorities, and commits to a strike with no human input at the moment of attack.

Why This Matters for the Middle East and Beyond

Iran's original Shahed design has already travelled far. Variants have turned up in conflicts across the Middle East — Houthi forces in Yemen have used them against shipping and Saudi infrastructure, and the platform has reportedly reached other non-state actors in the region. An AI-guided evolution of the same design would reshape the threat calculus for regional militaries and naval forces operating in contested airspace.

The prospect also piles pressure onto counter-drone developers. German startup Tytan is targeting production of 3,000 drone interceptors per month from a new factory. Finland's border-security consortium this week formalised agreements with Nokia Defence and 61N Solutions to field networked counter-drone capabilities on patrol vessels and vehicles. Both efforts represent the kind of industrial response analysts say will be essential if autonomous munitions spread.

The Autonomy Threshold

The core concern in Beskrestnov's assessment is the removal of the human from the targeting loop entirely. Current international norms and rules of engagement still assume some level of human authorisation before a lethal strike. A commercially accessible, mass-produced drone capable of making that call itself would put pressure on legal frameworks, escalation management, and air-defence architectures all at once.

For defence planners in the Gulf, Israel, and elsewhere already dealing with Shahed-derived threats, the warning from Kyiv reads as a direct preview of what they're likely to face within the next several years.

U.S. Transfers Four Ocean Aero Triton Surveillance Drones to Philippines Navy

Washington has handed over four dual-mode autonomous underwater and surface vehicles to Manila in a $13 million deal aimed at countering Chinese activity in the Philippine EEZ.

SECTOR · AMERICA PUBLISHED · 2026-06-25 HUMAN-VERIFIED

Washington Delivers $13M Drone Package to Philippine Navy

The United States has completed a $13 million transfer of four Ocean Aero Triton autonomous underwater and surface vehicles to the Armed Forces of the Philippines, a move that significantly upgrades Manila's ability to monitor Chinese maritime activity within its exclusive economic zone.

The handover took place on June 22 at the Philippine Navy's Naval Operating Base Subic, where U.S. and Philippine military officials gathered for a formal ceremony marking the delivery.

A Hybrid Platform with Unique Submersible Capability

The Triton system is unusual among uncrewed maritime platforms in that it can transition autonomously between surface sailing and submerged operation without requiring operator input. Propelled by wind and solar power, each vehicle can sustain missions for up to 30 days and carries a low radar signature designed to reduce its detectability.

For military applications, the platform can be configured with high-resolution video and thermal sensors, solid-state radar, passive towed arrays, and side-scan sonar — giving it utility across intelligence gathering, mine countermeasures, and anti-submarine warfare missions. Data is transmitted via a resilient mesh network that links the vessels' above- and below-surface sensor feeds.

Alliance Modernisation at the Core of the Transfer

Colonel Daniel Oh, the U.S. Embassy's Senior Defense Official and chief of the Joint U.S. Military Assistance Group-Philippines, described the transfer as part of a broader effort to help the Philippines shift toward territorial defense and build awareness in critical waterways, according to Marine News Magazine. He added that American assistance would extend to helping Manila integrate the systems into its operational and sustainment planning.

The delivery aligns with the bilateral Philippines-U.S. Security Sector Assistance Roadmap, which structures American military aid to the archipelago nation.

Unmanned Systems Testing Intensifies in the Region

The AUSV transfer comes alongside separate U.S. military experimentation with offensive uncrewed platforms in Philippine waters. Last month, U.S. Army Special Forces used an explosive-laden drone boat — based on the same design as Ukraine's Magura attack drone — to sink a decommissioned merchant vessel in the Strait of Luzon, according to naval analyst H.I. Sutton, as cited by The Maritime Executive. The warhead used was an explosively formed penetrator shaped charge engineered to cut through steel hulls.

Together, the exercises suggest Washington is actively testing a broader uncrewed maritime playbook in the Western Pacific, drawing tactical lessons from the Ukraine conflict and adapting them for potential contingencies in the South China Sea.

Why It Matters

The delivery of the Triton AUSVs represents a concrete step in the U.S. strategy of bolstering allied maritime capacity across the first island chain. By equipping the Philippines with persistent, low-observable surveillance assets, Washington is extending its sensor network into some of the most contested waters in the Indo-Pacific — without deploying its own forces directly. The pairing of surveillance capability with demonstrated offensive drone tactics nearby signals that the alliance is moving beyond rhetoric toward operational readiness.

China's J-35 Copies F-35 Hardware but Misses the Software Edge, Analysts Warn

A new analysis argues China's stealth aviation program has copied the physical form of U.S. fifth-generation fighters but failed to absorb the software-defined adaptability that makes them genuinely lethal.

SECTOR · CHINA PUBLISHED · 2026-06-25

China's Stealth Gamble: Hardware Without the Brain

China's J-35 looks like an F-35. According to a new Defense News analysis, that's roughly where the resemblance ends — and where the problems begin.

The J-35, Beijing's carrier-capable stealth fighter, bears obvious visual similarities to the U.S. F-35 Joint Strike Fighter. Analysts say Chinese engineers studied American stealth design principles closely, drawing on open-source data and, per longstanding U.S. government allegations, cyber-enabled espionage. But defense analyst Ecklebe, cited in the Defense News report, argues China absorbed the wrong lessons: the J-35 replicates the F-35's physical characteristics while piling too much emphasis on raw hardware performance and too little on the software-defined architecture and continuously updated tactics, techniques, and procedures that give the American jet its operational edge.

Integration Over Imitation

The analysis reaches beyond the J-35 itself to China's broader air defense posture, and the picture there complicates any simple assessment of Chinese vulnerability. The People's Liberation Army has built a deeply integrated, multi-layered defensive network — not a brittle system that stealth aircraft can easily pick apart.

"What makes China's defenses formidable is not any single radar or missile, but rather their integration into a unified IADS," Ecklebe warned, referring to China's integrated air defense system. Low-frequency early-warning radars, passive detection systems, and satellite sensors feed into shared command centers at the corps level — a structure built specifically to blunt the advantages stealth aircraft enjoy against individual sensor nodes.

The result is a strategic paradox: China has developed stealth fighters optimized against a style of air defense it has already moved past in its own territory.

Doctrine Gap Could Prove Costly

For Western defense planners, the practical implication is that a low radar cross-section and supersonic performance tell only part of the story. A stealth aircraft that can't adapt quickly to electronic warfare, shifting threat libraries, or real-time battlespace data may underperform badly in a contested environment regardless of how it looks on a spec sheet.

The F-35 program has invested heavily in its Autonomic Logistics Information System and a continuous software upgrade cycle, letting the aircraft evolve faster than adversaries can reverse-engineer its capabilities. If the J-35 lacks an equivalent update pipeline and software ecosystem, the capability gap between the two platforms could widen over time rather than close.

Why It Matters

China is accelerating naval aviation development, with the J-35 slated for deployment aboard its newer carrier classes. Whether Beijing has genuinely matched American fifth-generation capability or simply replicated its silhouette will directly shape the military balance in the Indo-Pacific. For U.S. allies in the region — Japan, the Philippines among them — that distinction carries real strategic weight.

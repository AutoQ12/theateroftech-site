China's J-35 Copies F-35 Hardware but Misses the Software Edge, Analysts Warn

A new analysis argues China's stealth aviation program has replicated the physical form of U.S. fifth-generation fighters while failing to absorb the software-defined adaptability that makes them truly capable.

SECTOR · CHINA PUBLISHED · 2026-06-25

China's Stealth Gamble: Hardware Without the Brain

China's efforts to replicate American fifth-generation stealth technology have produced aircraft that look the part — but may fall dangerously short in the ways that matter most in modern air combat, according to a new analysis published by Defense News.

The assessment centers on the J-35, Beijing's carrier-capable stealth fighter, which bears a striking visual resemblance to the U.S. F-35 Joint Strike Fighter. Analysts argue the similarity is more than cosmetic — Chinese engineers appear to have studied American stealth design principles closely, potentially drawing on both open-source data and, according to longstanding U.S. government allegations, cyber-enabled espionage.

Yet the core finding is that China absorbed the wrong lessons. According to defense analyst Ecklebe, cited in the Defense News report, the J-35 replicates the F-35's physical characteristics while placing excessive emphasis on raw hardware performance at the expense of the software-defined architecture and continuously updated tactics, techniques, and procedures that give the American platform its operational edge.

Integration Over Imitation

The analysis extends beyond individual platforms to China's broader air defense posture. Far from being a brittle system vulnerable to stealth penetration, the People's Liberation Army has built what analysts describe as a deeply integrated, multi-layered defensive network.

"What makes China's defenses formidable is not any single radar or missile, but rather their integration into a unified IADS," Ecklebe warned, referring to China's integrated air defense system. Low-frequency early-warning radars, passive detection systems, and satellite sensors are fused into shared command centers at the corps level — a structure specifically designed to reduce the advantages that stealth aircraft provide against individual sensor nodes.

This creates a strategic paradox: China has built stealth aircraft optimized against a style of air defense it has simultaneously moved beyond in its own territory.

Doctrine Gap Could Prove Costly

The implications are significant for how Western defense planners assess Chinese air power. A stealth aircraft that excels on paper — with low radar cross-section and supersonic performance — may underperform in contested environments if its mission systems cannot adapt quickly to electronic warfare, shifting threat libraries, or real-time battlespace data.

The U.S. F-35 program has invested heavily in its Autonomic Logistics Information System and continuous software upgrades, allowing the aircraft to evolve faster than adversaries can reverse-engineer its capabilities. If China's J-35 lacks an equivalent update cycle and software ecosystem, the gap between the two platforms may widen over time rather than close.

Why It Matters

As China accelerates naval aviation development — with the J-35 slated for deployment aboard its newer carrier classes — the question of whether Beijing has genuinely matched American fifth-generation capability or merely replicated its silhouette will directly shape the military balance in the Indo-Pacific. For U.S. allies in the region, from Japan to the Philippines, that distinction carries real strategic weight.

Teledyne FLIR 'First Look' Throwable Ground Drone Controlled by Black Hornet System

Teledyne FLIR Debuts Throwable Tracked Ground Drone at SOF Week

Teledyne FLIR has introduced a throwable tracked ground drone called the First Look, built to be hurled through windows or into confined spaces and operated through the same control interface soldiers already use with the company's Black Hornet nano-aerial drone.

The company showed the platform at SOF Week, the annual special operations industry gathering, pitching it directly at dismounted units that need fast, low-signature reconnaissance in urban buildings and tunnel networks.

Shared Control Architecture With Black Hornet

The First Look's most consequential feature may be what it shares with an existing product. By using the same controller and operator interface already fielded with the Black Hornet, Teledyne FLIR cuts the training burden: soldiers familiar with the aerial system can move to the ground robot without starting from scratch.

The Black Hornet itself has earned wide adoption across NATO and partner militaries for its palm-sized form factor and quiet performance during close-target reconnaissance. Extending that control architecture to a ground platform reflects a broader industry drive toward multi-domain unmanned teaming at the individual soldier level.

Throwable Form Factor Targets Close-Quarter Missions

The tracked design is built to absorb the shock of being thrown into a room or dropped from height, giving a small team an immediate view of a space before anyone enters. That has direct applications in hostage rescue, building clearance, and counter-IED tasks.

The debut comes as demand for rugged, low-cost ground robots climbs across Western special operations communities, driven partly by lessons from the Ukraine conflict, where unmanned systems across every category have reshaped tactical reconnaissance and attack dynamics.

Why It Matters

The First Look reflects a shift among leading manufacturers away from standalone platforms and toward interoperable families of air and ground robots that share hardware, software, and logistics chains. For special operations forces already carrying the Black Hornet, a compatible ground drone reduces procurement friction and strengthens the case for unified multi-domain reconnaissance at the squad level. With close-quarters combat scenarios growing more common in modern conflict, demand for compact, rugged ground ISR tools is likely to keep rising.

Teledyne FLIR 'First Look' Throwable Ground Drone Controlled by Black Hornet System

Teledyne FLIR has unveiled a rugged, throwable tracked ground drone that shares its control architecture with the company's established Black Hornet miniature aerial platform.

SECTOR · UNMANNED VEHICLES PUBLISHED · 2026-06-23

Teledyne FLIR Debuts Throwable Tracked Ground Drone at SOF Week

Teledyne FLIR has introduced a new terrestrial unmanned platform — dubbed the First Look — designed to be thrown through windows or into confined spaces and operated using the same control interface already familiar to soldiers from the company's widely fielded Black Hornet nano-aerial drone.

The tracked drone was showcased at SOF Week, the annual special operations industry gathering, positioning it squarely at the small-unit reconnaissance market where dismounted forces require rapid, low-signature situational awareness in complex environments such as urban buildings and tunnel networks.

Shared Control Architecture With Black Hornet

A defining feature of the First Look is its interoperability with Teledyne FLIR's existing Black Hornet ecosystem. By leveraging the same controller and operator interface already in service with allied militaries, the company reduces training burden — soldiers who already operate the Black Hornet aerial drone can transition to the ground system with minimal relearning.

The Black Hornet has achieved broad adoption across NATO and partner militaries for its palm-sized form factor and quiet operation during close-target reconnaissance missions. Extending that control paradigm to a ground platform reflects a broader industry push toward multi-domain unmanned teaming at the individual soldier level.

Throwable Form Factor Targets Close-Quarter Missions

The tracked design is intended to survive the physical shock of being thrown into a room or dropped from a height, giving small teams an immediate view of a space before entry — a capability with direct applications in hostage rescue, building clearance, and counter-improvised explosive device tasks.

The debut comes as demand for rugged, low-cost ground robots surges across Western special operations communities, driven in part by lessons observed in the Ukraine conflict, where unmanned systems of every category have reshaped tactical reconnaissance and attack dynamics.

Why It Matters

The First Look illustrates a maturing trend: rather than developing standalone unmanned platforms, leading manufacturers are now building interoperable families of air and ground robots that share hardware, software, and logistics chains. For special operations forces already equipped with the Black Hornet, a compatible ground drone reduces procurement friction and strengthens the case for unified multi-domain reconnaissance at the squad level. As close-quarters combat scenarios grow more prevalent in modern conflict, demand for compact, rugged ground ISR tools is likely to accelerate.

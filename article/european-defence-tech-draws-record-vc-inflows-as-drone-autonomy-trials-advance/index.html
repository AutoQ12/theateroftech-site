European Defence Tech Draws Record VC Inflows as Drone Autonomy Trials Advance

Venture capital flowing into defence technology has surpassed $12 billion globally, with European start-ups and autonomous drone programmes increasingly attracting investor and military attention.

SECTOR · EUROPE PUBLISHED · 2026-06-23

Global Defence VC Tops $12 Billion as Europe Races to Catch Up

Venture capital investment in defence technology has surpassed $12 billion, according to data cited by Private Equity Wire, with capital increasingly concentrated in drones, autonomous naval systems and space-based surveillance. The surge reflects a broader reorientation of private markets toward security-related sectors, driven in part by the war in Ukraine and mounting pressure on European governments to rebuild their defence industrial bases.

European companies are attracting a growing share of this attention, albeit well behind their American counterparts in absolute terms. AI-focused defence firm Helsing and hardware developer Stark are among the European start-ups pulling in substantial funding rounds as the continent attempts to close the gap with established US players.

Industry observers note that investor enthusiasm is becoming more disciplined. Rather than backing speculative growth stories, capital allocators are now prioritising companies with demonstrable government procurement pipelines, revenue visibility and long-term strategic contracts — a sign that the sector is maturing beyond its early hype cycle.

Autonomous Drone Teaming Moves From Concept to Flight Test

On the technology side, a landmark autonomy trial has underscored how rapidly the operational landscape is shifting. Leonardo's M-346 jet trainer has been paired with Baykar's Kizilelma combat drone in autonomous formation flight trials, according to The War Zone and Aviation International News. The demonstration positions the Kizilelma — one of only a handful of fighter-type unmanned combat air vehicles to have reached flying hardware — as a credible collaborative combat aircraft (CCA) candidate for export markets.

The trials carry particular relevance for Europe, where the collapse of the pan-European Future Combat Air System effort has sharpened the focus on CCA capabilities. Germany, for instance, has a stated requirement to field an operational CCA before 2030. While the Kizilelma itself may not be a direct contender for European air forces, the autonomous AI teaming technology it is now demonstrating could prove attractive to a range of customers.

For Leonardo, the exercise also reinforces its position as a potential integrator of drone-commander capabilities for platforms such as the Eurofighter Typhoon, in which the Italian firm holds a stake through its involvement in the Eurofighter consortium.

New Hardware Expands the European Drone Ecosystem

Beyond the high-profile autonomy trials, a steady flow of smaller hardware milestones is broadening Europe's unmanned systems landscape. Danish start-up Acodyne secured pre-seed funding for an electric cargo drone family, with flight testing targeted before the end of 2026, according to Aviation International News. Meanwhile, ERC unveiled its Victor hybrid-electric VTOL cargo drone at the ILA 2026 air show in Berlin, and Schiebel's Camcopter S-300 was selected for anti-submarine evaluation under the European SWORD project.

Why It Matters

The convergence of record private investment, maturing autonomous flight technology and a busy hardware pipeline points to a European defence tech sector in genuine structural transition. For investors and policymakers alike, the central question is no longer whether the capital is there — it clearly is — but whether European start-ups can translate funding and flight tests into programmes of record before US and Turkish competitors consolidate their leads.

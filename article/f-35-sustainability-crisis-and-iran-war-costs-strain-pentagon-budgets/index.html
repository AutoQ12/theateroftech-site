F-35 Sustainability Crisis and Iran War Costs Strain Pentagon Budgets

The F-35 program's top officer admits the fleet has outgrown its supply chain, while an $87.6 billion Iran war supplemental and inter-service budget fights expose deepening U.S. defense affordability pressures.

SECTOR · AMERICA PUBLISHED · 2026-06-26

F-35 Fleet Growth Has Outrun Its Own Supply Chain

The U.S. military's most expensive weapons program is facing a self-inflicted readiness crisis. Lt. Gen. Gregory L. Masiello, the F-35 program executive officer, told a Senate subcommittee that the Pentagon failed to build up adequate spare parts and components to support the growing joint strike fighter fleet, according to Defense News. "We didn't put enough parts and pieces on the shelf," Masiello said. The admission underscores a recurring tension in major acquisition programs: procurement momentum consistently outpacing the logistics architecture needed to sustain it.

The warning arrives at a moment of acute fiscal stress across the U.S. defense enterprise, with multiple competing budget pressures threatening to crowd out investment in long-term readiness.

White House Requests $87.6 Billion Iran War Package

On June 24, the White House formally submitted an $87.6 billion supplemental funding request to Congress, the bulk of which — roughly $67.15 billion — is earmarked for the Pentagon to cover operational and materiel costs from the ongoing conflict with Iran, according to Aviation Week. The request reflects both the direct consumption of munitions and platforms and the accelerated wear on fleet assets drawn into the fight.

Among the less visible consequences of those operations is a documented surge in flight hours for the U.S. Navy's E-2D Advanced Hawkeye, which has been pressed into overland airborne early warning roles that would traditionally fall to larger Air Force platforms.

Pentagon Robs E-2D Account to Rescue E-7 Wedgetail

The budget maneuvering around airborne early warning aircraft has become particularly tangled. After initially moving to cancel the Air Force's E-7 Wedgetail acquisition, Pentagon and Air Force leadership reversed course, according to The War Zone. To fund the reversal, the White House Office of Management and Budget approved pulling $650 million from the Navy's aircraft procurement account — specifically funds slated for new E-2D Hawkeyes — alongside nearly $899 million from a classified Air Force procurement line.

The Navy had only recently outlined a plan to purchase 12 new E-2Ds across fiscal years 2027 through 2029, explicitly to replace airframes whose service lives are being burned down faster than planned due to current operational demands. The House Appropriations Committee has since pushed back on the proposed E-2D cut, setting up a confrontation with the White House over how to sustain two overlapping early warning fleets simultaneously.

A Broader Affordability Reckoning

These developments collectively illustrate a structural problem in U.S. defense planning. The Pentagon's proposed fiscal year 2027 budget already requests record levels of investment — including $63 billion for autonomous systems and $58.5 billion for AI, according to Washington Technology — yet the Iran conflict is generating emergency costs that existing top-line funding was never designed to absorb.

The F-35 sustainability shortfall, the E-2D funding raid, and the scale of the Iran supplemental all point to the same underlying tension: a force built around high-end, high-cost platforms is struggling to remain both operationally relevant and financially sustainable when called upon at wartime tempo.

Why it matters: The convergence of a major fleet readiness gap in the F-35 program, inter-service budget conflicts over critical early warning aircraft, and a $67 billion war bill arriving outside the regular budget cycle signals that U.S. defense affordability challenges are no longer theoretical — they are actively shaping operational choices.

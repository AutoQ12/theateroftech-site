F-35 Sustainability Crisis and Iran War Costs Strain Pentagon Budgets

The F-35 program's top officer admits the fleet has outgrown its supply chain, while an $87.6 billion Iran war supplemental and inter-service budget fights expose deepening U.S. defense affordability pressures.

SECTOR · AMERICA PUBLISHED · 2026-06-26

F-35 Fleet Growth Has Outrun Its Own Supply Chain

The U.S. military's most expensive weapons program has a readiness problem of its own making. Lt. Gen. Gregory L. Masiello, the F-35 program executive officer, told a Senate subcommittee that the Pentagon never built up enough spare parts and components to support the growing joint strike fighter fleet. "We didn't put enough parts and pieces on the shelf," Masiello said, according to Defense News. It's a blunt admission that procurement kept moving while the logistics architecture needed to sustain the fleet did not.

That admission lands at a moment when several competing budget pressures are eating into the Pentagon's room to maneuver on long-term readiness.

White House Requests $87.6 Billion Iran War Package

On June 24, the White House formally submitted an $87.6 billion supplemental funding request to Congress. The bulk of it — roughly $67.15 billion — goes to the Pentagon to cover operational and materiel costs from the ongoing conflict with Iran, according to Aviation Week. The request captures both direct munitions and platform consumption and the accelerated wear on fleet assets pulled into the fight.

One of the less visible costs is a documented surge in flight hours for the U.S. Navy's E-2D Advanced Hawkeye, which has been pressed into overland airborne early warning roles that would traditionally belong to larger Air Force platforms.

Pentagon Robs E-2D Account to Rescue E-7 Wedgetail

The budget maneuvering around airborne early warning aircraft has become particularly tangled. After initially moving to cancel the Air Force's E-7 Wedgetail acquisition, Pentagon and Air Force leadership reversed course, according to The War Zone. To pay for that reversal, the White House Office of Management and Budget approved pulling $650 million from the Navy's aircraft procurement account — money earmarked for new E-2D Hawkeyes — alongside nearly $899 million from a classified Air Force procurement line.

The timing is awkward. The Navy had only recently laid out a plan to buy 12 new E-2Ds across fiscal years 2027 through 2029, specifically to replace airframes whose service lives are being burned down faster than planned by current operational demands. The House Appropriations Committee has since pushed back on the proposed E-2D cut, setting up a confrontation with the White House over how to sustain two overlapping early warning fleets at once.

A Broader Affordability Reckoning

Taken together, these episodes point to a structural problem in U.S. defense planning. The Pentagon's proposed fiscal year 2027 budget already requests record investment levels — including $63 billion for autonomous systems and $58.5 billion for AI, according to Washington Technology — yet the Iran conflict is generating emergency costs that the existing top line was never built to absorb.

The F-35 sustainability shortfall, the raid on E-2D procurement, and the scale of the Iran supplemental all trace back to the same tension: a force built around high-end, high-cost platforms that is struggling to stay both operationally relevant and financially sustainable at wartime tempo.

Why it matters: A major fleet readiness gap in the F-35 program, inter-service budget fights over critical early warning aircraft, and a $67 billion war bill arriving outside the regular budget cycle together signal that U.S. defense affordability challenges are no longer theoretical — they are actively shaping operational choices.

Defence Tech VC Funding Tops $12bn as European Startups Chase US Giants

A surge in venture capital flowing into defence technology has already surpassed last year's full-year total, with European firms competing for a share of a market dominated by billion-dollar US raises.

SECTOR · EUROPE PUBLISHED · 2026-06-23

Global venture capital investment in defence technology has exceeded $12 billion so far in 2026, surpassing the entire previous year's total well before the calendar turns, according to market data cited by Private Equity Wire — a pace that underscores how dramatically the sector's investment profile has shifted since Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine.

The headline figure has been driven largely by outsized American financing rounds. Anduril Industries anchored the boom with a $5 billion raise that pushed its valuation to approximately $61 billion — nearly double its prior mark. Autonomous systems developers Saronic Technologies and Shield AI, focused on uncrewed maritime and aerial platforms respectively, also featured prominently among significant recipients.

Europe is a smaller but growing part of the picture. Startups including Helsing and Stark have drawn meaningful capital as governments across the continent press to modernise ageing defence industrial bases. The subsectors attracting the most attention on both sides of the Atlantic are broadly consistent: drone systems, autonomous naval platforms and space-based surveillance technology, where valuations have climbed sharply as investors compete for assets with credible growth trajectories.

Yet the mood among allocators is maturing. Industry participants note a shift away from purely speculative bets toward companies with demonstrable revenue, established government procurement relationships and the prospect of long-term strategic contracts — a sign that the defence tech investment cycle may be entering a more disciplined phase even as overall volumes remain elevated.

The funding surge coincides with fresh evidence of European ambitions in autonomous combat systems. Turkey's Kizilelma fighter drone — one of only a handful of fighter-class uncrewed combat air vehicles to have produced actual flying hardware — has been conducting teaming trials with the M-346 fighter-trainer, demonstrating the kind of AI-enabled autonomy that analysts say could attract export interest across Europe and beyond. Notably, the Kizilelma is not subject to US International Traffic in Arms Regulations, giving it potential access to markets that American or ITAR-adjacent systems cannot reach, according to The War Zone.

On the intelligence side of the market, US startup HighGround has secured $6.5 million in seed funding to build an AI-driven platform aggregating hundreds of federal procurement data sources, aiming to help investors anticipate where Pentagon and broader defence spending will flow next.

Why it matters: The combination of record-pace venture funding, maturing investor discipline and accelerating hardware development — particularly in Europe and Turkey — points to a defence technology ecosystem that is expanding both in scale and sophistication. For European governments grappling with urgent rearmament timelines, the question is increasingly whether domestic startups can convert investor enthusiasm into the kind of sovereign production capacity that strategic independence requires.

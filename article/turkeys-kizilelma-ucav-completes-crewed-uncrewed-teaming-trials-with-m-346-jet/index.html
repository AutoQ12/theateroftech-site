Turkey's Kizilelma UCAV Completes Crewed-Uncrewed Teaming Trials With M-346 Jet

Baykar's combat drone has conducted autonomous formation flight alongside a Leonardo M-346 trainer jet, placing Turkey among a handful of nations pursuing advanced crewed-uncrewed teaming capabilities.

SECTOR · MIDDLE EAST PUBLISHED · 2026-06-23 HUMAN-VERIFIED

Turkey has taken a significant step forward in military aviation autonomy after its Kizilelma unmanned combat air vehicle completed airborne teaming trials with a Leonardo M-346 fighter-trainer jet, according to reporting from The War Zone and Aviation International News. The exercise, referred to as the K-Swarm trial, saw the M-346 act as a lead aircraft directing the autonomous UCAV in coordinated formation flight — a capability previously demonstrated publicly only by the United States and China.

The milestone consolidates Turkey's position as a serious player in what defence circles call crewed-uncrewed teaming, or CUT — a concept central to next-generation air combat doctrine. The U.S. Air Force is accelerating its own Collaborative Combat Aircraft programme along similar lines, while China has also prioritised the technology. Turkey's entry into this domain signals that the gap between major powers and ambitious mid-tier defence producers is narrowing faster than many analysts anticipated.

The Kizilelma's trajectory has been remarkable. Late last year, Turkey demonstrated that the aircraft could fire a domestically produced Gökdoğan radar-guided air-to-air missile to destroy a target drone — reportedly the first time a UCAV had ever launched such a weapon operationally. Days afterwards, Boeing's MQ-28A Ghost Bat replicated the feat using an AIM-120 AMRAAM during trials in southern Australia, suggesting the two programmes are developing in close parallel.

A critical factor driving export interest in the Kizilelma is its independence from U.S. International Traffic in Arms Regulations. ITAR restrictions have long constrained which countries can acquire advanced Western military platforms, but Turkish-origin systems carry no such limitations. Ankara has already exploited this advantage across its broader drone portfolio, securing deals with states that would be unable — politically or technologically — to obtain comparable American or European systems.

The teaming trial also underscores the growing breadth of Turkey's unmanned portfolio. Separately, Turkish firm Zyrone has fielded the PUHU-KBRN, a UAS designed for decontamination operations in chemical, biological, radiological and nuclear environments. According to Janes, the system entered service with Turkish forces in 2025 and made its first public appearance during Exercise EFES-2026 in Izmir last month. Carrying up to 30 kilograms of decontamination fluid, it can operate up to 15 kilometres from safe areas.

Why it matters: The Kizilelma teaming trial is not merely a technical achievement — it signals that Turkey is building a credible, exportable ecosystem of advanced drone capabilities largely free from Western regulatory constraints. For countries in the Middle East, Africa, and beyond seeking sovereign-capable air power without geopolitical strings attached, Ankara's growing portfolio is becoming an increasingly compelling proposition.

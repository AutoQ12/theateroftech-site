Baykar's Kizilelma combat drone has flown autonomous formation sorties alongside a Leonardo M-346 trainer jet, putting Turkey in rare company as one of the few nations to have demonstrated crewed-uncrewed teaming in the air.

Turkey's Kizilelma Completes Crewed-Uncrewed Teaming Trial With M-346 Jet

Baykar's Kizilelma unmanned combat air vehicle has completed airborne teaming trials with a Leonardo M-346 fighter-trainer jet, according to reporting from The War Zone and Aviation International News. The exercise, called the K-Swarm trial, had the M-346 fly as lead aircraft while directing the autonomous UCAV through coordinated formation flight — a capability only the United States and China had previously demonstrated publicly.

That puts Turkey in a short list of countries pursuing crewed-uncrewed teaming, or CUT, which has become central to next-generation air combat doctrine. The U.S. Air Force is pushing ahead with its own Collaborative Combat Aircraft programme along similar lines, and China has made the technology a priority. Turkey's entry suggests the gap between major powers and ambitious mid-tier defence producers is closing faster than many analysts had expected.

The Kizilelma's recent test record is hard to ignore. Late last year Turkey demonstrated that the aircraft could fire a domestically produced Gökdoğan radar-guided air-to-air missile and destroy a target drone — reportedly the first time a UCAV had launched such a weapon operationally. Within days, Boeing's MQ-28A Ghost Bat repeated the feat using an AIM-120 AMRAAM during trials in southern Australia, suggesting the two programmes are moving on roughly parallel tracks.

A key factor shaping export demand for the Kizilelma is its clean bill of health under U.S. International Traffic in Arms Regulations. ITAR restrictions have long determined which countries can acquire advanced Western platforms, but Turkish-origin systems carry none of those constraints. Ankara has already turned that into a commercial advantage across its broader drone portfolio, signing deals with states that couldn't — politically or practically — obtain comparable American or European hardware.

The teaming trial also illustrates how wide Turkey's unmanned portfolio has grown. Separately, Turkish firm Zyrone has fielded the PUHU-KBRN, a UAS built for decontamination work in chemical, biological, radiological and nuclear environments. According to Janes, the system entered service with Turkish forces in 2025 and made its first public appearance during Exercise EFES-2026 in Izmir last month. It carries up to 30 kilograms of decontamination fluid and can operate up to 15 kilometres from safe areas.

Why it matters: The Kizilelma teaming trial signals that Turkey is assembling a credible, exportable ecosystem of advanced drone capabilities that sits largely outside Western regulatory reach. For countries in the Middle East, Africa, and beyond that want sovereign-capable air power without geopolitical conditions attached, Ankara's growing portfolio is becoming a harder offer to pass up.

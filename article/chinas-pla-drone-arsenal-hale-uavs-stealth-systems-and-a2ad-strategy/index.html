China's PLA Drone Arsenal: HALE UAVs, Stealth Systems, and A2/AD Strategy

A congressional commission review details how China's defense industry has built a broad portfolio of advanced unmanned systems designed to challenge U.S. stealth assets and extend PLA reach across the Indo-Pacific.

China Builds Broad Military UAV Portfolio Targeting U.S. Stealth Advantage

China's defense industry has developed a wide-ranging portfolio of advanced unmanned aerial systems — including high-altitude surveillance platforms, supersonic stealth drones, and long-range strike vehicles — as the People's Liberation Army seeks to erode U.S. technological advantages in the Indo-Pacific, according to testimony submitted to the U.S.-China Economic and Security Review Commission.

The assessment, drawing on open-source research into Chinese defense programs, describes an accelerating effort by major Chinese aerospace conglomerates to leverage existing aviation and missile expertise in building sophisticated UAV capabilities across multiple mission categories.

HALE Platforms and Anti-Stealth Capabilities

Among the systems highlighted is the Shendiao, or "Divine Eagle," a twin-fuselage high-altitude long-endurance UAV developed by the Shenyang Aircraft Design Institute. The platform is reported to carry high-performance anti-stealth radar systems capable of detecting low-observable aircraft, a capability that could directly threaten U.S. stealth platforms operating in contested airspace. Open-source reporting indicated the aircraft completed its first flight in early 2015.

The Divine Eagle is assessed as a potential enabler of China's anti-access/area-denial strategy, which is designed to complicate U.S. military operations and force projection across the Western Pacific.

Supersonic Stealth UAV Under Development

Also attributed to the Shenyang institute is the Anjian, known in translation as the "Dark Sword" — a supersonic stealth UAV that was first publicly revealed at an earlier stage of development. The program underscores the PLA's ambition to field unmanned platforms that combine speed and low observability, characteristics traditionally associated only with crewed combat aircraft.

Strike Drones Expanding Export and Domestic Roles

On the strike side, the Yilong series — comparable in role and weight class to the U.S. MQ-9 Reaper — has been developed for both PLA Air Force service and export. The Xianglong, or "Soar Dragon," a HALE UAV produced by the Guizhou Aviation Aircraft Corporation and designed in Chengdu, was among the earlier systems revealed publicly, first appearing at the Zhuhai Airshow in 2006 before continuing through an extended development cycle.

The Lijian, a flying-wing stealth combat drone, is separately reported to have achieved development milestones as recently as 2016, according to the commission review.

Why It Matters

The breadth of China's unmanned programs — spanning stealth strike, supersonic flight, anti-stealth surveillance, and HALE endurance — reflects a deliberate, multi-vector strategy to contest air dominance without symmetric investment in crewed fifth-generation fighters alone. As the U.S. military expands its own autonomous systems across the Indo-Pacific, including unmanned surface vessels and collaborative combat aircraft, China's parallel drone buildup adds urgency to Washington's force modernization calculus. The commission's findings remain relevant as both powers race to field autonomous systems that could define the character of future high-end conflict.

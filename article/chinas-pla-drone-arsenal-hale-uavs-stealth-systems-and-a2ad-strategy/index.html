China's PLA Drone Arsenal: HALE UAVs, Stealth Systems, and A2/AD Strategy

A congressional commission review details how China's defense industry has built a broad portfolio of advanced unmanned systems designed to challenge U.S. stealth assets and extend PLA reach across the Indo-Pacific.

SECTOR · CHINA PUBLISHED · 2026-06-23

China's Military UAV Push Targets U.S. Stealth Advantage

China has assembled a wide-ranging portfolio of military drones — high-altitude surveillance platforms, supersonic stealth vehicles, long-range strike systems — aimed squarely at eroding U.S. technological advantages in the Indo-Pacific. That's the picture emerging from testimony submitted to the U.S.-China Economic and Security Review Commission, which draws on open-source research into Chinese defense programs.

The assessment describes major Chinese aerospace conglomerates leveraging existing aviation and missile expertise to push into sophisticated UAV development across multiple mission categories, and doing so at pace.

HALE Platforms and Anti-Stealth Radar

One of the more consequential systems in the review is the Shendiao — the "Divine Eagle" — a twin-fuselage high-altitude long-endurance UAV built by the Shenyang Aircraft Design Institute. It reportedly carries high-performance anti-stealth radar capable of detecting low-observable aircraft, which would put U.S. stealth platforms at direct risk in contested airspace. Open-source reporting placed its first flight in early 2015.

The Divine Eagle is assessed as a potential enabler of China's anti-access/area-denial strategy, the architecture designed to complicate U.S. force projection across the Western Pacific.

Supersonic Stealth Drone

Also out of Shenyang is the Anjian, or "Dark Sword" — a supersonic stealth UAV that surfaced publicly at an earlier stage of development. The program reflects PLA ambitions to put speed and low observability into the same unmanned airframe, a combination previously confined to crewed combat aircraft.

Strike Drones for Home Use and Export

On the strike side, the Yilong series sits in roughly the same role and weight class as the U.S. MQ-9 Reaper and has been developed for both PLA Air Force service and overseas customers. The Xianglong, or "Soar Dragon" — a HALE UAV produced by Guizhou Aviation Aircraft Corporation and designed in Chengdu — appeared publicly as far back as the 2006 Zhuhai Airshow before working through an extended development cycle.

The Lijian, a flying-wing stealth combat drone, is separately reported to have hit development milestones as recently as 2016, per the commission review.

Why It Matters

The breadth of China's unmanned programs — stealth strike, supersonic flight, anti-stealth surveillance, HALE endurance — reflects a deliberate, multi-vector effort to contest air dominance without betting everything on crewed fifth-generation fighters. As the U.S. military expands its own autonomous systems across the Indo-Pacific, including unmanned surface vessels and collaborative combat aircraft, China's parallel drone buildup adds pressure to Washington's force modernization calculus. The commission's findings carry weight as both powers race to field autonomous systems that could define how a high-end conflict actually unfolds.

Kawasaki and Airbus Partner to Develop Eurodrone ASW Variant for Japan

Kawasaki Heavy Industries and Airbus have signed an MOU to study whether the Eurodrone MALE UAV can be turned into an anti-submarine warfare platform shaped around Japan's Pacific maritime needs.

SECTOR · EUROPE PUBLISHED · 2026-06-26

Kawasaki and Airbus Sign MOU on Maritime Eurodrone Variant

Kawasaki Heavy Industries and Airbus Defence & Space have agreed to jointly examine whether the European U950 Eurodrone medium-altitude long-endurance platform could be adapted into a dedicated maritime surveillance and anti-submarine warfare (ASW) asset for Japan's naval forces.

The memorandum of understanding, announced on 26 June, sets up a framework for further talks on designing, developing, and potentially commercialising a Japanese maritime variant of the system. Under the arrangement, a Eurodrone-based platform would carry Japanese-developed sensors and effectors built for ASW missions in the Pacific region, according to FlightGlobal.

Strategic Rationale for Both Sides

The partnership carries mutual benefits that reach well beyond Japan's immediate operational needs. Airbus has indicated the collaboration could generate significant advantages for prospective European naval variants of the Eurodrone too — an application several European navies have been eyeing as unmanned maritime patrol capabilities gain strategic urgency.

For Kawasaki, the arrangement offers a route to integrate domestically developed mission systems into a mature Western airframe, potentially accelerating capability delivery for the Japan Maritime Self-Defense Force, which faces intensifying submarine threats in regional waters.

Japan's Growing Role in the Eurodrone Programme

Tokyo's engagement with the Eurodrone programme isn't new. Japan's Acquisition, Technology & Logistics Agency has held observer status on the project since 2023, and earlier this month extended that arrangement, signalling sustained institutional interest ahead of any formal procurement decisions.

The core Eurodrone programme remains a four-nation European effort involving France, Germany, Italy, and Spain. India also holds observer status. First flight of a prototype is currently scheduled for 2029, meaning any Japanese maritime derivative would follow a longer development timeline still to be defined through the new MOU process.

Europe-Japan Defence Industrial Convergence

The agreement reflects a wider trend of European and Indo-Pacific defence industries deepening ties as both regions accelerate rearmament. European defence investment has surged markedly since early 2025, with the EU's €150 billion SAFE loan programme now conditioning major contracts on rapid, bloc-sourced production scale-up, according to Defense News.

Japan, meanwhile, has been diversifying its defence partnerships beyond the United States, actively seeking co-development arrangements with European firms across domains ranging from fighter aircraft to unmanned systems.

Why It Matters

A successful Eurodrone ASW variant would be one of the most substantive Europe-Japan defence co-development projects to date, demonstrating that the Eurodrone has export and adaptation potential beyond its original four-nation customer base. It would also give the programme a commercial validation at a critical point — before the first prototype has even flown. How quickly the two companies can turn the MOU into concrete design studies will determine whether Japan becomes a genuine partner in shaping the next generation of European unmanned maritime patrol capability.

Poland Acquires Shield AI V-BAT Drones for Baltic Sea Naval Operations

Warsaw has contracted California-based Shield AI to supply V-BAT unmanned systems to the Polish Navy, deepening U.S.-Polish defense ties as Poland eyes a role in Shield AI's autonomous X-BAT fighter jet programme.

SECTOR · EUROPE PUBLISHED · 2026-06-25 HUMAN-VERIFIED

Poland Contracts Shield AI for Naval Drone Capability

The Polish Ministry of National Defence has signed a contract with California-based Shield AI to procure the company's V-BAT vertical take-off and landing drone for the Polish Navy, according to Defense News. The acquisition is designed to strengthen Warsaw's maritime surveillance and operational reach across the Baltic Sea, a body of water that has taken on heightened strategic importance since Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine.

The deal adds Poland to a growing list of NATO allies investing in autonomous naval unmanned systems. The V-BAT, which requires no runway for launch or recovery, is well suited to shipborne operations in contested littoral environments.

X-BAT Manufacturing Offer Adds Industrial Dimension

The procurement carries additional strategic weight beyond the immediate hardware purchase. According to Defense News, Polish Prime Minister Donald Tusk has revealed that Shield AI offered Poland a manufacturing role in its X-BAT programme — an autonomous vertical take-off fighter jet still in development. If realised, that arrangement would embed Poland more deeply in next-generation U.S. autonomous combat aviation, contributing sovereign industrial capacity while gaining access to cutting-edge technology.

The dual announcement — a near-term operational purchase paired with a longer-term industrial partnership offer — reflects a wider pattern across European defence, where procurement decisions are increasingly tied to domestic production stakes and technology transfer.

Baltic Security Drives Unmanned Investment

Poland has steadily expanded its defence budget and modernisation agenda in recent years, with Baltic Sea security a consistent priority. The V-BAT contract fits within that framework, giving the Polish Navy an organic unmanned aerial surveillance asset capable of operating from surface vessels without dedicated flight-deck infrastructure.

The timing also aligns with a broader surge in European defence investment. The FTSE 350 Aerospace & Defence Index climbed more than 85 percent between January 2025 and June 2026, according to Defense News, while venture funding into European defence startups reached €7.4 billion in 2025 alone. The EU's €150 billion SAFE loan programme is simultaneously pushing member states to scale up domestic and intra-European production.

Why It Matters

Poland's V-BAT acquisition is a relatively contained but symbolically significant step: it demonstrates that NATO's eastern flank is not merely relying on legacy platforms to address evolving maritime threats. The potential manufacturing role in the X-BAT programme, if formalised, would mark a deeper industrial integration between Poland and a leading U.S. autonomous systems developer — precisely the kind of sovereign capability partnership that European defence planners have been advocating as the continent accelerates its rearmament.

Japan Procures Australian Drone40 Loitering Munition for Island Defense

Japan's Ground Self-Defense Force has picked DefendTex's Drone40 loitering munition for short-range strike missions, with deliveries through Japanese trading firm Marubeni Aerospace due by May 2027.

SECTOR · UNMANNED VEHICLES PUBLISHED · 2026-06-26

Japan Formally Selects Drone40 for Ground Forces

Japan has awarded a contract for the Drone40 loitering munition, built by Australian firm DefendTex, to sharpen the Ground Self-Defense Force's (JGSDF) strike punch across its dispersed island territories. The selection was confirmed in a Japan Ministry of Defense (MoD) statement reported by Janes. The contract went to Japanese trading company Marubeni Aerospace in late March, with all units scheduled to reach Japanese hands by late May 2027.

A ministry spokesperson said the system had completed testing and cleared the procurement threshold.

A Pocket-Sized Weapon With Tactical Reach

Drone40 is small enough to hold in one hand — 13 cm long, weighing up to 400 grams at maximum take-off weight — but it reaches out to 35 km and can stay airborne for up to an hour. Launch options range from hand-throw to vehicle, boat, aircraft, or a standard 40 mm grenade launcher, giving ground units real flexibility in how they put it to work.

The system uses GPS-based autopilot guidance and ties back to a portable ground control station through an encrypted radio data-link, according to company specifications cited by Janes.

Remote Islands and Landing Threats

Tokyo's stated rationale ties the procurement directly to its southwestern island defense strategy. The MoD said the weapon is intended to engage invading forces at close range to friendly units, counter amphibious landing operations on remote islands, and protect critical infrastructure — missions that map squarely onto Japan's longstanding concerns about its outlying Ryukyu island chain.

The buy also fits Japan's broader defense posture since the 2022 National Security Strategy authorized counterstrike capabilities and pushed the defense budget sharply higher. Loitering munitions offer an affordable, scalable complement to the longer-range precision weapons Japan is developing and procuring at the same time.

Australia-Japan Defense Industrial Ties

Choosing an Australian platform carries diplomatic weight. Tokyo and Canberra have built closer defense ties under their Reciprocal Access Agreement, and this contract adds an industrial layer to that relationship. DefendTex, based in Melbourne, demonstrated the Drone40 to the Australian Army in 2021.

Why It Matters

Japan's Drone40 purchase shows how mid-tier democratic allies are moving quickly to put low-cost loitering munitions into frontline ground units — a shift driven in large part by battlefield lessons from Ukraine. For DefendTex, winning a contract with one of Asia's most capable militaries delivers commercial validation and raises the company's profile in an Indo-Pacific defense market that is growing fast.

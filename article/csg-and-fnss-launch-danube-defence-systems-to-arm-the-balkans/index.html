CSG and FNSS Launch Danube Defence Systems to Arm the Balkans

Croatian firm CSG and Turkish manufacturer FNSS have launched a joint venture called Danube Defence Systems to build armoured vehicles, including a medium tank, on European soil for Balkan and regional buyers.

SECTOR · EUROPE PUBLISHED · 2026-06-23

CSG and FNSS Launch Danube Defence Systems at Eurosatory 2026

Croatia's Consolidated Savings Group and Turkish armoured vehicle manufacturer FNSS used Eurosatory 2026 in Paris to announce a joint venture called Danube Defence Systems, according to Janes. The Paris defence show ran 15–19 June.

The venture's headline product is the Karpat medium tank, developed under the collaboration and set to be manufactured in Europe. Danube Defence Systems plans to build out a full family of Karpat variants — armoured personnel carriers and infantry fighting vehicles — and will also sell FNSS's existing wheeled and tracked platforms alongside the company's weapon systems.

The intended customer base is European, with a particular focus on the Balkans, where several nations are running active armour modernisation programmes tied to NATO commitments and the lessons absorbed from Russia's war in Ukraine. Croatia has been upgrading its ground forces; Serbia, Slovenia, and North Macedonia are working through varying timelines for platform replacement across their armoured fleets.

FNSS already exports tracked and wheeled vehicles to the Middle East, Southeast Asia, and parts of Europe. Teaming with a Croatian company gives the Turkish manufacturer a direct presence inside the EU's defence industrial base — commercially useful at a moment when EU procurement frameworks increasingly favour European-origin production.

The timing matters for another reason. The EU's Security Action for Europe (SAFE) financing mechanism is taking shape as a key funding route for member-state defence purchases, and Balkan EU members and candidates are actively exploring access to it. European manufacturing credentials could help Danube Defence Systems qualify for or benefit from those programmes.

What the Eurosatory announcement didn't include: contracts, letters of intent with specific buyers, or confirmation of where in Europe the manufacturing will actually take place.

Why it matters: Danube Defence Systems fits a pattern of non-EU defence firms seeking European production partnerships to get inside a rapidly growing market. For Balkan states that need armour and need it soon, a regionally anchored supplier offering a medium tank family and associated platforms could look like a credible alternative to the longer lead times that come with the larger Western European primes.
